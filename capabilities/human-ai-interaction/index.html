Capability / Interaction Design

Human-AI Interaction

Designing the loops, signals, and intervention points that let people collaborate with AI instead of merely receiving outputs.

Trust and legibility Confidence cues Human control patterns

Focus Areas

Interaction patterns that help people understand what the system is doing, how certain it is, and how to steer it.

  • Conversation design for open-ended intent
  • Uncertainty communication and confidence states
  • Editable inputs, overrides, and recovery paths
  • Feedback loops that improve user trust

Artificial intelligence introduces a fundamentally different type of interaction compared to traditional software.

Most software behaves deterministically. A button produces a predictable result. A form submission triggers a defined process. AI systems behave differently because they rely on probability, inference, and pattern recognition.

Designing interaction for these systems requires a shift in mindset. Instead of designing control flows, the task becomes designing collaboration between humans and intelligent systems.

From Commands to Conversations

Traditional interfaces are command-driven. Users provide explicit instructions, and the system executes them.

AI systems introduce a conversational layer. Users often begin with incomplete intentions, vague questions, or exploratory ideas, and the system helps refine those intentions through dialogue.

The interface must support this exploratory process rather than forcing users into rigid workflows.

Designing for Uncertainty

One of the most important challenges in AI interaction design is uncertainty. AI systems do not always know the correct answer. They estimate probabilities based on patterns in data, and their responses may carry varying levels of confidence.

A well-designed interface must communicate this uncertainty clearly so users understand when the system is confident, when it is making a best guess, and when human judgment is required.

Maintaining Human Control

As AI systems become more capable, it becomes increasingly important to maintain a sense of user control.

Users often want to guide the system, refine its outputs, or override its decisions. Design should therefore support intervention through editable prompts, adjustable goals, and explicit correction points.

Designing the Feedback Loop

Human-AI interaction is rarely a single action. A user expresses an intention, the AI interprets it, the system produces an output, and the user evaluates the result before providing feedback.

Designing this loop carefully is essential. Each step should provide clear signals about what the system understood, what it attempted, and how the user can adjust the outcome.

Building Trust Through Interaction

Trust is not created by claims about accuracy or intelligence. Trust is built through experience.

When users understand how a system behaves, when they see how their input influences outcomes, and when they can correct mistakes easily, trust emerges naturally and the relationship becomes collaborative rather than mechanical.
